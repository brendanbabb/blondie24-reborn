What am I looking at?  (how the 2-second evolve works)

A live re-implementation of Chellapilla & Fogel's 1999 evolutionary checkers experiment: a 1,743-weight feedforward network that learns to play checkers with no hand-crafted evaluation, no opening book, no endgame database, and no backpropagation — only self-play under an evolutionary strategy. Source on GitHub.

Every AI turn, here's what happens in roughly two seconds:

  1. The AI holds a population of 6 networks. Each plays 3 self-play games against random opponents from the group. Training search goes 4 plies deep — same depth the AI uses against you, and the same depth the paper used. No hand-crafted endgame boost: Fogel didn't use one either.
  2. Games are scored +1 win / 0 draw / −2 loss (paper's asymmetric scoring). The top 3 networks survive; the bottom 3 are replaced by Gaussian-mutated offspring of the survivors.
  3. That's one generation. The browser runs as many gens as fit into those two seconds — typically 4–10 at these settings.
  4. Time's up. The current top-ranked network runs depth-4 minimax from the board you see and plays a move. The snapshot is frozen — it doesn't change while you think.

Evolution is paused during your turn, so the AI you face at any point was the fittest in the last-completed tournament. Across ~30 AI turns in a game, the population runs through ~100–200 generations — a meaningful fraction of what the paper used to reach Class-A play. Watch the generation counter climb and the network-architecture panel's connections shift as evolution reshapes the weights.
