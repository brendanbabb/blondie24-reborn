Blondie24 Reborn

A checkers opponent that evolves while it plays you.

What am I looking at?  (how the 1-second evolve works)

A live re-implementation of Chellapilla & Fogel's 1999 evolutionary checkers experiment: a 1,743-weight feedforward network that learns to play checkers with no hand-crafted evaluation, no opening book, no endgame database, and no backpropagation — only self-play under an evolutionary strategy. Source on GitHub.

Every AI turn, here's what happens in roughly one second:

  1. The AI holds a population of 6 networks. Each plays 3 self-play games against random opponents from the group.
  2. Games are scored +1 win / 0 draw / −2 loss. The top 3 networks survive; the bottom 3 are replaced by Gaussian-mutated offspring of the survivors.
  3. That's one generation. The browser runs as many gens as it can fit into the second — typically 5–20 here. The Python version on a laptop runs ~3–5 gens/second at the same settings.
  4. When the second is up, the current top-ranked network is handed depth-4 minimax and plays the move against you. The snapshot is frozen — it won't change while you think.

Evolution is paused during your turn, so the AI you face at any point was the fittest in the last-completed tournament. Across ~30 AI turns in a game, the population typically runs through 200–500 generations — on the order of what the paper used to reach Class-A play.
